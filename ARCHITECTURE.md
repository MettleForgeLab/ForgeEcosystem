## Overview

The Forge Ecosystem defines the ecological and governance architecture within which Forge-based language systems operate.

It establishes layered separation between:

- Training geometry
- Runtime switching
- Deployment membrane constraints
- Governance and stewardship

No single layer is sufficient alone.

Stability emerges from layered interaction.

## Layer Responsibilities

### 1. Training Geometry

Defines optimization posture prior to deployment.

- Shapes switching attractor
- Encodes non-inference discipline
- Penalizes authority posture projection
- Rewards calibrated expansion

Training geometry does not enforce deployment constraints.

---

### 2. Switching Policy

Implements runtime behavior regulation.

- Estimates structural state variables
- Selects response mode
- Applies hysteresis
- Regulates expansion and contraction
- Detects marginal novelty collapse

Switching logic operates within membrane constraints.

---

### 3. Deployment Membrane

Enforces non-negotiable constraints.

- Constitutional layer invariants
- Use-case containment
- Identity inference prohibition
- Persona restriction
- Tool surface gating

Membrane constraints override switching decisions.

---

### 4. Governance Layer

Maintains longitudinal stability.

- Perturbation-based basin depth measurement
- Audit surfaces and traceability
- Version discipline
- Threshold immutability
- Distributed governance posture

Governance prevents slow drift under incentive pressure.

## Architectural Commitments

The Forge Ecosystem is grounded in:

- Boundary-first design
- Non-inference
- Non-authority stance
- Stateless operation
- Use-case containment
- Distributed governance
- Stewardship over ownership

These commitments are structural and non-overrideable within canonical scope.

## Stability Principle

Conditional boundedness defines how a system behaves.  
The Forge Ecosystem defines the environment that preserves that behavior over time.

Engine and ecology are distinct but inseparable.
